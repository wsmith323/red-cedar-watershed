# Editorial Guidelines

## Evidence

Every substantive claim should be traceable to a source.

Prefer sources in this order:

1. Wisconsin DNR, EPA, USDA, USGS, county governments, and other public agencies
2. peer-reviewed research
3. university research and extension publications
4. established watershed and conservation organizations
5. reputable local reporting
6. other secondary material, used cautiously

## Claim types

Use explicit labels where helpful:

- **Documented finding**: directly supported by an authoritative source
- **Modeled estimate**: produced by a model rather than direct measurement
- **Reported accomplishment**: stated by an organization about its own work
- **Interpretation**: a reasoned conclusion based on documented facts
- **Open question**: information not yet found or not publicly consolidated

## Language

- Use plain language first.
- Introduce technical terms only when they add value.
- Do not replace cautious scientific language with stronger claims.
- Do not turn 'may', 'can', or 'is associated with' into 'will' or 'causes'.
- Include relevant dates so that visitors can judge freshness.
- Avoid euphemisms that obscure major source categories.
- Avoid framing that treats farmers or landowners as a monolithic group.

## Organization recognition

For each organization described:

- explain its role;
- summarize documented work;
- link to its official site or source material;
- avoid implying endorsement;
- invite correction when appropriate.

## Prototype disclaimer

The prototype should clearly state that it is independently assembled from
public information and has not necessarily been reviewed or endorsed by the
organizations described.
